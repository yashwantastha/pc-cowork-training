⚠️ Three Things to Know Before You Touch Anything

It can sound sure of itself and still be wrong. These tools are prediction engines, not truth engines. They can get a name wrong, misread a policy, or invent a detail that sounds real, confidently. That's why every task ends with you checking it before you send or act. Your new core skill isn't writing, it's auditing.
It should ask before it acts. Claude and Gemini are meant to ask for approval before sending an email, posting a message, or changing anything outside the chat. If you don't see an approval step, that's unusual, stop and flag it. Be explicit in your instruction ("draft this, don't send it").
🔒 Never paste employee or candidate personal data into the chat. No names tied to salaries, no ID numbers, no health, disciplinary, disability, ethnicity, religion or union information. This is Special Category Data under GDPR and it must not go into a prompt. The People team handles the most sensitive data in the company, so the Governance tab is not optional reading for us, it's the first tab that matters.

Phase 1 · Foundational Literacy

📚 From "Search Box" to "Digital Colleague"

Most of us learned AI as a better search engine: type a question, get an answer. That mental model works for Gemini and quick lookups, but it's exactly what makes Cowork confusing at first. Cowork wants an outcome and access ("here's a folder, produce a 30-60-90 onboarding plan"), then it plans and executes multiple steps. Same underlying model, very different way of working.

The one idea to hold onto: generative AI is a probabilistic prediction engine, not a deterministic truth engine. It predicts a likely-good answer. Usually excellent, occasionally confidently wrong. That single fact explains why we verify everything and keep a human in the loop.

The Prompt Framework: R·T·F·C·G

The first output is rarely perfect, and that's what discourages people. A repeatable structure fixes most of it. Define five things and vague requests turn into reliable outputs:

ElementWhat to specifyPeople-team example
RoleWho the AI should act as"You are an experienced People Ops partner at a global company."
TaskThe exact job to do"Draft a 30-60-90 day onboarding checklist."
FormatShape of the output"As a Markdown table with a column for owner and due week."
ContextThe background it needs"Based on the attached job description and our onboarding policy."
GuardrailsWhat it must / must not do"Don't invent policies; if something isn't in the docs, flag it. No employee names."
Role: You are an experienced People Ops partner. Task: Draft a 30-60-90 day onboarding checklist for the role in the attached JD. Format: A Markdown table, columns = Milestone | Owner | Target week. Context: Use only the attached JD and our onboarding policy in this folder. Guardrails: Don't invent any policy. Flag anything missing rather than guessing. Use the role title, never a person's name.

Anthropic and Google both reward this. It's the single habit that most improves your results, and it's what the hands-on recipes are built on.

Tokens, Context & Markdown, in Plain Language

A token is a small chunk of text, roughly a syllable. The AI reads and writes in tokens. Rough rules of thumb:

  • 1 page of a dense doc (~400 words) ≈ 500–600 tokens
  • A 20-page policy document ≈ 10,000–12,000 tokens

Context is simply everything the assistant is holding in mind right now, your instruction, the files it opened, earlier messages, connected data it pulled in. A .md (Markdown) file is just a plain-text document with light formatting (# for headings, - for bullets); Cowork often hands back .md because it's clean and reusable.

Why People teams should care about context: when you hand Cowork a folder, everything in it becomes context. That's powerful (it can reason across the handbook + a JD at once) and a responsibility (don't put a payroll export in a folder you've granted access to). More in Governance.

Phase 2 · Workflow Application

🔌 Connectors: How It Reaches Your Apps

A connector is a secure "bridge" that lets Claude or Gemini see one of your other apps, your inbox, calendar, Drive, and for Claude, Slack. Without one, the tool only knows what you type. You connect via a standard sign-in (OAuth) and grant permission, no coding.

One rule that beats every feature: a connector only shows what you could already see yourself. Connecting it to a channel or drive you can't access doesn't unlock anything. And default to read-only access until you fully trust a workflow.

Already enabled for this session, Gemini Enterprise: Google Workspace, GitHub, Asana. Claude: Slack, Gmail, Drive, Calendar. If one won't authorize, it's usually the org toggle, flag it.

Gemini Gems vs Claude Projects (the migration trap)

If you've used Gemini "Gems", Claude "Projects" look similar but handle memory differently, and mixing up the two causes most early frustration:

Gemini Gems

Lightweight. Link a live Google Drive file and it always references the latest version without re-uploading. Great for convenience; limited for heavy analysis.

Claude Projects

A persistent workspace where you upload a corpus once, e.g. five policy documents, set standing instructions ("always answer in DH tone, cite the policy"), and every chat in that Project already has that context.

For People work: put your handbook, leave policy, and onboarding guide into one Project, set the tone once, and every "what's our policy on X" question starts from that context instead of a blank page.

Skills & the "Important Folder"

Skills are reusable, packaged capabilities, like camera presets. You pick one and it handles the details (e.g. producing a polished Word or Excel file). Some are built in; your admins may push custom ones, check the Plugins panel to see what's available to the People team.

The "important folder" is the practical safety habit: when Cowork works on your files, you grant access to one dedicated folder, not your whole computer.

  • One folder per task or project.
  • Grant access to only that folder.
  • Never point it at HR records, payroll, or personnel files "just in case".

Same idea as giving a contractor a key to one room, not the whole building. This matters more for us than most teams, because of what our folders contain.

Phase 3 · Agentic Automation

🤖 Delegating a Whole Task to Cowork

This is where the "manage the AI as it works" mindset pays off. A Cowork task isn't one message, it's the tool reading files, planning steps, drafting, checking, and revising, then handing you a result to review. Your job shifts from typing to describing the outcome, granting access, and auditing the plan and output.

  1. Create a dedicated folder for the task and put the inputs in it.
  2. Describe the end state clearly (use R·T·F·C·G).
  3. Keep it in "Ask before acting" mode so it pauses before doing anything real.
  4. Review the plan it proposes before you click Approve.
  5. Check the output against the source before you use it.

Scheduled Background Tasks

Cowork can run recurring tasks on a schedule, even when your laptop is closed. The classic pattern is a morning digest. A People-team version: every Monday at 8:00, summarize new joiners starting this week from the shared tracker and draft welcome notes for review.

Automate the drafting, never the deciding. A scheduled task can prepare and draft; it should not send anything sensitive or make a people decision on its own. Always leave the final review to a human.

Plugins

Plugins bundle connectors, skills and shortcuts into a role-specific toolset, so you inherit an optimized workflow instead of building prompts from scratch. Rather than memorizing anything, browse the Plugins panel and see what's installed for the People team.

A note on honesty: if you read about a ready-made "HR plugin" with one-click commands for offers or onboarding, treat it as "check whether it's actually installed for us" rather than assuming it exists. The recipes in the next tab work today using plain Cowork, no special plugin required, and you can swap in a plugin later if one is available.

🍳 How to Read a "Recipe"

Each recipe maps to a painful, repetitive People-team task. The pattern is always the same: a co-facilitator runs it live, then you replicate it with your own (non-sensitive) sample data while support is in the room. Every recipe ends with a verification step, because that's the job now.

The three are deliberately drawn from different P&C functions so everyone sees something familiar: Recipe 1 is a People Ops job, Recipe 2 is a Total Rewards job, and Recipe 3 is shared knowledge work any function does. The technique is identical across all of them, so whichever you run, it transfers to your own work.

Use sample / anonymized data in the workshop. For practice, strip real names and any personal identifiers. Use "Candidate A", a fake start date, a placeholder salary band. The skill transfers perfectly; the risk doesn't.
Recipe 1 · 30-60-90 Day Onboarding Checklist
"Building a tailored onboarding plan for every new role, from scratch, every time."

Tool: Claude Cowork · Inputs: the role's job description + your onboarding policy/guide, in one folder.

  1. Put the JD and the onboarding policy into a dedicated folder; grant Cowork access to just that folder.
  2. Prompt it with R·T·F·C·G (below).
  3. Review the draft, correct anything that drifted from the policy, then export to your template.
Role: You are an experienced People Ops partner. Task: Create a 30-60-90 day onboarding plan for the role in the attached JD. Format: Markdown table, columns = Milestone | Owner | Target week | Source (which policy section it came from). Context: Use only the JD and onboarding policy in this folder. Guardrails: Don't invent steps or policies. Flag gaps as "TBC - confirm with manager". Use the role title, never a person's name.

Verify: spot-check three rows against the policy's "Source" column. If a row has no source, it's a hallucination candidate, cut or confirm it.

Recipe 2 · Offer Letter & Job Description Drafting
"Re-typing near-identical offer letters and JDs, then re-checking them for consistency."

Tool: Claude Chat or Cowork · Inputs: your approved offer/JD template + structured, non-personal details (role, level, comp band, location, start date).

  1. Provide the template and the structured fields, use a placeholder like "[CANDIDATE NAME]" rather than a real name.
  2. Ask it to fill the template and flag anything missing.
  3. Human-review comp figures against the source band before anything leaves the building.
Role: You are a Total Rewards specialist. Task: Draft an offer letter from the attached template using these fields: [role], [level], [comp band placeholder], [location], [start date]. Use [CANDIDATE NAME] as a placeholder. Format: Follow the template exactly; return only the filled letter. Context: The template and our offer guidelines in this folder. Guardrails: Do not invent numbers or clauses. If a field is missing, insert "[MISSING - confirm]". No real personal data.
Compensation needs human validation. AI can be unreliable on pay figures, especially senior roles. Treat every number as "confirm against the source band", never as final.

Verify: check every bracketed field is resolved, and that the comp figure matches the approved band before signing/sending.

Recipe 3 · Policy & Handbook Q&A + People-Team Comms
"Answering the same handbook questions all day, and re-drafting the same All-Hands updates."

Tool: Claude Project (for Q&A) or Chat (for comms) · Inputs: your current HR policy documents.

  1. Create a Project, upload the current policies, and set a standing instruction: "Answer only from these documents, quote the relevant section, and say 'not covered' if it isn't there."
  2. Ask real questions ("What's the notice period for probation?") and confirm it cites the section.
  3. For comms, ask it to draft the update in DH's voice from bullet points, then edit for tone.
Role: You are a People Ops knowledge assistant. Task: Answer the employee's question about our policy. Format: A short answer, then "Source: [policy name, section]". Context: Use ONLY the documents uploaded to this Project. Guardrails: If the answer isn't in the documents, say "Not covered, please check with your P&C partner." Never guess.

Verify: the "Source" line lets anyone trace the answer back. No source = don't rely on it. Keep policies in the Project up to date.

Cowork Settings · Trusted Folders & Global Instructions

Settings → Cowork. Two settings here save real time.

Trusted folders

Normally every Cowork session asks you to confirm folder access. Marking a folder trusted skips that dialog for that one folder. Practical move: set your People-team working folder as trusted once, and you won't re-approve it every session.

Only trust folders you'd always be happy to grant. Never mark an HR-records, payroll, or personnel folder as trusted, that confirmation dialog is a deliberate reminder of what Claude can see. (See Governance.)

Global instructions

Text that applies automatically to every Cowork task, so you don't re-explain it each time. Good uses for People work:

  • Output rules, e.g. "always start a document with a one-line summary"
  • File naming, e.g. "name outputs YYYY-MM-DD_description"
  • A standing guardrail, e.g. "never include real employee names, use placeholders" and "ask before creating new files"

Think of it as a one-time briefing for a new contractor: write it once, it works every session.

Also Lives in Settings

Customize → Connectors is where you check connector status and add new ones (you used this in Try It Live). Customize → Plugins shows what's installed for the team. General → "Instructions for Claude" is like Global instructions but also applies to Chat, a good place for your role and how you like output formatted (no credentials or personal data).

🛡️ Governance & Data Privacy (read this one twice)

The People team handles the most sensitive data in the company, so these guardrails aren't bureaucracy, they're what let us use these tools at all. Under the EU AI Act, everyone using AI systems also needs baseline AI literacy, which is part of why this session exists.

🚫 The Bright Lines for People Work

No autonomous hiring or termination decisions. AI must not screen out candidates, rank people for hiring, or decide on dismissals on its own. Using AI to make or automate those decisions is a high-risk / prohibited pattern under the EU AI Act and exposes DH to serious liability. AI can help you prepare and organize; a human decides. Always.
No Special Category Data in prompts. Health, disability, ethnicity, religion, sexual orientation, union membership, biometric data (GDPR Article 9), plus salaries tied to names, IDs, and disciplinary records. These never go into a chat, a connected folder, or an uploaded file.
Compensation analysis needs human validation. AI can be confidently wrong on pay, especially senior bands. Every figure is "confirm against the source", never final.

What's OK vs Not OK to Process

Generally OK (with human review before use)Do not process
Anonymized / placeholder dataSpecial Category Data (GDPR Art. 9)
Published JDs, policies, handbooksSalaries or performance tied to named individuals
Draft comms, templates, general researchUnreleased financials / compensation planning
Operational workflows & checklistsRaw personnel files, disciplinary or health records

If you're unsure which side something falls on, treat it as "do not process" and check with your P&C partner or Legal/DPO.

Operational Hygiene for Agentic Tools

  • "Ask before acting" mode, especially with any file that came from outside DH. It forces a pause for your approval before any real action.
  • Isolated folders, use a dedicated "playground" folder. Never grant Cowork access to HR records, payroll, or shared drives full of personnel data.
  • Execution auditing, read the plan before you Approve. If a step diverges from your intent, stop it and flag it.
  • Prompt-injection awareness, a malicious instruction can be hidden inside an uploaded document (e.g. a CV or a PDF from the web) trying to trick the AI into leaking or sending data. Another reason to keep "ask before acting" on and to review outputs.

Accounts & Training Data

Use only the org-approved enterprise account for any work data. On enterprise/business terms, your prompts are not used to train the models and the org is covered by a GDPR-compliant data agreement.

Never use a personal Claude/Gemini account for DH data. Personal consumer tiers can retain data and may use it for training. If you're ever unsure what tier you're on or what the exact retention terms are, confirm with IT/Legal before processing anything, don't assume.

FAQ

Is my Cowork credit shared or per person?
Per person, $1,000 each for the pilot through September, then a monthly budget from October (~$50, or ~$100 for some functions).
Does reading a Slack channel or inbox burn a lot of credit?
No, reading is cheap (a busy channel is a few thousand tokens, cents). What adds up is running many multi-step Cowork tasks all day, or re-reading the same large source repeatedly. Batch related asks into one session.
The AI gave me a confident answer, do I still need to check it?
Yes, every time. Confidence is not accuracy. For People work especially, trace answers back to a source before you rely on them or send them.
What if I accidentally pasted something sensitive?
Stop, don't continue the task, and flag it to your workshop lead / IT. Don't try to "fix" it by pasting more. Going forward, use placeholders and keep sensitive files out of connected folders.
Do I need Claude Code?
Almost certainly not, it's the engineer-focused surface. Chat and Cowork cover People-team work. If a future task genuinely needs bulk file processing, ask an AI Champion first.

Quiz Pause Points (12 questions, 2 per content tab)

The portal has a built-in self-check score. You don't need to run all 12 live, mixing keeps energy up:

  • Run live as a group vote (show of hands / poll, then reveal): Phase 1 Q2, both Phase 2 questions, Compare Q1, and both Governance questions. These carry the ideas people most need to retain.
  • Leave self-serve (people answer as they read the tab): the rest. Encourage them to try before revealing.
Facilitator tip: after a wrong-answer reveal, ask "why is that the trap?" out loud, the discussion sticks better than the correct answer alone.

Live Q&A Spots

  • After Phase 2 (Workflows) — connectors and permissions generate the most questions; hold ~5 min here.
  • During "Try It Live" — the connect-and-compare block is the key hands-on hand-off; a co-facilitator owns unblocking connector authorizations (usually an org toggle, not user error).
  • After the HR Recipes hands-on — 2–3 volunteers share what they built; this doubles as Q&A and social proof.

Co-Facilitator Roles

  • Lead: drives the shared screen, demos, and keeps time.
  • Co-facilitator 1: watches the room and chat, fields questions, flags any connector-authorization issues (usually an org toggle, not user error), and runs the live quiz votes.
  • Co-facilitator 2 (if available): circulates during the hands-on block, unblocks people one-to-one, and lines up share-out volunteers.

With ~20 attendees, two circulating helpers during the hands-on block is the single biggest driver of "everyone finishes one real task."
